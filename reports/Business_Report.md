# Project Wafa — Business Report

**Falcon Bank UAE · Customer Retention Intelligence Platform**

---

## 1. Executive summary

During a wave of expatriate relocation, Falcon Bank's 18-person CX team faces a
surge of multilingual messages, many carrying churn intent. Project Wafa triages
each message end-to-end in under a second on a laptop: it reads the message (in
English, Arabic, Hindi, or Tagalog), estimates churn risk by fusing message
signals with account behaviour, and recommends a **transparent** retention action
with a **human-reviewed** draft reply. Confirmed leavers are routed to a
**dignified goodbye** — never retention pressure.

On the provided synthetic data the models are highly accurate (TF-IDF text
classifiers reach 1.00; a fine-tuned DistilBERT matches on churn at 1.00 and reaches
0.984 on issue type; the churn model reaches ROC-AUC 1.00). We treat this as a
**property of the data, not proof of production quality** — the generator
deliberately encodes strong, near-separable churn drivers (see §8).

## 2. Dataset overview

- **252 messages** — balanced 36 per issue type across 7 issue types; balanced 84
  each across churn signals High/Medium/Low. Languages: English 138, Arabic 47,
  Tagalog 38, Hindi 29.
- **240 customers** — 11 behavioural features + `nationality_region` (protected) +
  `churned` label. Overall churn rate **28.8%**. By segment: Premium 34.3%,
  Mass 26.8%, Private 23.8%. Segments: Mass 149, Premium 70, Private 21.
- **Synthetic** — generated by `data/generate_wafa_data.py`, which bakes in and
  balances the churn/fairness structure.

## 3. Message insights (from EDA)

- The message set is perfectly class-balanced by design, so class imbalance is not
  a modelling concern here (it will be in production).
- Non-English messages are 45% of volume (Arabic + Hindi + Tagalog), confirming
  that a monolingual pipeline would leave nearly half the queue untriaged — the
  multilingual route is essential, not cosmetic.
- Account-closure and remittance messages concentrate the highest churn signals,
  matching the relocation scenario.

## 4. Customer churn drivers (from feature importance)

Permutation importance on the held-out set (churn model):

| Feature | Importance |
|---|---|
| `balance_trend_3m` | **0.334** (dominant) |
| `intl_transfer_spike` | 0.0005 |
| `avg_balance_aed` | ~0.0001 |
| others | ≈ 0 |

**Reading:** a sharply declining 3-month balance is by far the strongest churn
signal, consistent with customers winding down accounts before leaving. Because
one feature dominates so strongly, the model is effectively a near-threshold rule
on balance trend — another symptom of the synthetic generator (§8).

## 4b. Trained model vs zero-shot LLM (evidence-based model choice)

We benchmarked our trained classifiers against a zero-shot LLM (Qwen2.5-0.5B) on the
same 42-message sample, classifying issue type:

| Approach | Accuracy | Macro-F1 |
|---|---|---|
| TF-IDF + LogReg (trained) | 1.00 | 1.00 |
| DistilBERT (fine-tuned) | 1.00 | 1.00 |
| Qwen2.5-0.5B (zero-shot, no training) | 0.55 | 0.50 |

The zero-shot LLM is also uneven across languages (English 0.64 down to **Hindi 0.00**).
**Business implication:** the trained, owned models are not just more accurate here —
they are faster, cheaper, run privately on the bank's own hardware, and behave
consistently across languages. Prompting a general LLM would be both worse and riskier
for a bank. (Honest caveat: the sample overlaps training data, so the fair trained
reference is the held-out ≥0.98 — still far ahead of zero-shot.)

## 5. Segment-level risk (Portfolio Overview)

Running the churn model + fusion across all 240 customers:

| Segment | Low | Medium | High |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mass | 95 | 24 | 30 |
| Premium | 44 | 9 | 17 |
| Private | 15 | 3 | 3 |
| **Total** | **154** | **36** | **50** |

Top risk drivers among High-band customers: `balance_trend_3m` (50),
`intl_transfer_spike` (41), `salary_credit_active` stopped (41),
`complaints_6m` (17).

**Takeaway for the team:** 50 High-risk customers need attention now. Premium
carries disproportionate High-risk share (17/70 ≈ 24%) and higher CLV, so it is
where relationship-manager time earns the most.

## 6. Retention strategy recommendations

1. **Protect Premium/Private High-risk first** — route to a Relationship Manager
   Call; these customers clear the CLV bar for personal outreach.
2. **Fee complaints + elevated risk → Fee Waiver / Service Recovery**, but only
   where CLV ≥ AED 5,000; below that, service support is fairer and cheaper.
3. **Remittance & relocation queries → practical help**, not discounts — these
   customers mostly want a smooth transaction, not an incentive.
4. **Confirmed leavers → Dignified Goodbye** — preserve goodwill and the option to
   return; spend zero retention budget on them.
5. **Cap every offer at 5% of CLV** — enforced in code, auditable per decision.

## 7. Estimated business impact (retention economics)

Illustrative, using platform outputs and a 5%-of-CLV offer cap:

- 50 High-risk customers. If their average CLV is ~AED 30,000, the **maximum**
  total offer exposure is 50 × 5% × 30,000 = **AED 75,000**.
- Retaining even **one-third** (≈17 customers) preserves ≈ 17 × 30,000 =
  **AED 510,000** of CLV — a ~6.8× return on the capped offer budget, before
  counting cheaper service-only saves.
- **Team-capacity math:** at ~4 minutes of human review per Wafa-triaged case, one
  agent can clear ~120 cases/day. The platform turns "read every message" into
  "review a ranked, pre-drafted shortlist," which is the actual bottleneck for an
  18-person team facing thousands of messages.

## 8. Stated limitations (read before trusting the numbers)

- **Synthetic data.** `generate_wafa_data.py` encodes churn drivers strongly and
  balances classes, so **1.00 accuracy is expected and does not transfer** to real,
  noisy data. One feature (`balance_trend_3m`) dominates the churn model.
- **Small size** (252 / 240 rows) — metrics have wide confidence intervals.
- **Text models trained on raw/short multilingual text** in the default laptop path
  (no translation downloads); non-English **confidence** is lower even when accuracy
  is saturated (see Ethics Statement §multilingual fairness).
- The platform is a **decision-support** tool. It recommends; humans decide and act.

## 9. What to build next

1. Real labelled production data + drift monitoring; re-check that
   `balance_trend_3m` isn't the only signal once noise is present.
2. Calibrated probabilities (Platt/isotonic) so `final_risk_score` is a real
   probability, not just an ordering.
3. Wire the multilingual-native embedding route as an A/B alternative to
   translate-then-classify and measure per-language quality directly.
4. Feedback loop: use human approve/reject/override decisions from the audit log as
   weak labels to improve the models.
